What I do

My research examines what drives public opinion, media consumption, and voting. Seeking to understand what attributes of the economy people value the most, my ongoing work focuses on explaining and predicting economic sentiment around the world with disciplined approaches to model selection. I also study the trajectories of voters’ beliefs by using survey data augmented with behavioral outdomes, such as respondents’ action on social media.

I am a Ph.D. candidate in the Department of Politics at NYU, a Research Associate at the Social Media and Political Participation Lab, and I have served as a Data Fellow at NYU’s College of Arts and Sciences.

Research Interests

I am interested in how actions of political influencers affect voters’s lives and decisions. I study how people with political objectives convey economic data, compete for office, and how they apply persuasion strategies in an environment where much of the electorate lacks interest in politics.

As political discourse permeates into citizen’s screens and pockets, many observers worry that permanent campaigning, populist rhetoric, and echo chambers are eroding democracy. My work shows that satisfaction with democracy has not decreased over the last decade: young Europeans do not evaluate democracy more critically than older Europeans, suggesting that young people are no more interested in non-democratic alternatives than older individuals.

Even if democracy is durable, social conflict could arise if some groups feel the system is not working for them, or if they resent fellow citizens. To better understand these phenomena, my other published and ongoing work asks the following questions:

  1. Can machine learning help us identify which aspects of the economy people value the most?
  2. Do citizens remember issue-based promises from election campaigns?
  3. Are American users of social media exposed to diverse viewpoints?
  4. To what degree was there overlap between the appeal of Donald Trump’s and Bernie Sanders’ messages?
  5. How likely are Americans who avoid hard news to encounter political information? My co-authors and I document involvement of cultural elites (singers, actors, etc.) in political debates and campaigns to find out.


I am passionate about understanding the connection between politics and economics through rigorous research. Before joining NYU, I have worked on projects related to discrimination, inclusive capitalism, and behavioral economics at MIT, the University of Chicago, and at the Peterson Institute for International Economics.

As a Data Fellow at NYU, I have advised undergraduate students on research design, data manipulation, and interpreting empirical results. I strive to guide students to become clear communicators especially in courses where we inspect large datasets. Earlier, I was a Teaching Fellow in the economics department at Harvard, and have taught courses covering topics including model selection, false discovery rates, classification and decision trees, the bootstrap and cross-validation.
